## About Me

I am an atmospheric scientist and Graduate Research Assistant in the Department of Earth, Environmental, and Atmospheric Sciences at Western Kentucky University, where my research focuses on severe-storm environments, climate variability, and data-driven atmospheric prediction.

My current work investigates environments supportive of tornadoes, large hail, and damaging winds across the Ohio River Basin using ERA5 reanalysis, NOAA Storm Events, SPC proximity soundings, and downscaled CMIP6 climate projections. I develop probabilistic frameworks for severe-weather occurrence using machine-learning methods, event–environment matching, and diagnostic analysis of thermodynamic and kinematic atmospheric predictors.

I also conduct large-scale atmospheric data processing on national high-performance computing systems, including NCAR Derecho, NCAR Casper, and TACC Stampede3, building reproducible scientific workflows for multi-terabyte climate and weather datasets.

My broader research interests include:

- Severe Convective Storm Climatology
- Numerical Weather Prediction (WRF/WPS)
- Climate Modeling and Downscaling
- Hydroclimatology and Precipitation Extremes
- Climate Extremes and Risk Assessment
- Machine Learning for Atmospheric Science

## Current Research

### Severe-Storm Environment Climatology and Future Projections
Developing a 30-year severe-storm environmental climatology across the Ohio River Basin using ERA5-derived atmospheric diagnostics and future CMIP6 climate projections.

### Probabilistic Severe Hazard Prediction
Building Random Forest classification workflows linking atmospheric environmental predictors with NOAA Storm Events to estimate tornado, large hail, and damaging-wind occurrence probabilities.

### Numerical Weather Prediction
Applying WRF/WPS simulations to investigate extreme weather events, including mesoscale convective systems and flash-flood-producing environments.

### Hydroclimatology
Analyzing precipitation-frequency characteristics using Kentucky Mesonet observations and NOAA Atlas 14 for engineering and hydroclimate applications.
